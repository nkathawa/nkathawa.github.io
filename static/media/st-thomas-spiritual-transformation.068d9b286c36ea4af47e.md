Thomas, oftentimes referred to as “doubting Thomas,” is certainly one of the most relatable disciples. As ordinary Christians, we are often faced with doubts, or, at the very least, questions. We desire to know things “for certain.” Almost paradoxically, the beauty of the Christian life is that it is simultaneously mystery and material. It’s not meant to be understood in the way we understand the world about us, but we are meant to be transformed, shaped, and molded by the truth of the Gospel message. At bottom, it’s only when the truth and beauty of who Jesus is and what He did on the cross sink into and penetrate our hearts, that we truly understand it all. And the Lord desires this for all of us, and will meet us where we are, transforming us by both little things and great miracles. In all of this, we can look to St. Thomas the Apostle as a template, an ordinary man who, almost in spite of his desire to know the truth concretely, came to know it deep in his heart and paid it all upon the martyr’s scaffold.



The images below are from the three-part mural at St. Thomas the Apostle Catholic Church in Ann Arbor, Michigan. My meditation will go through each one (along with a bonus image), touching on the heart of each of St. Thomas’s encounters with the Lord.


## Thomas Touches the Wounds of the Risen Christ

![Thomas Touches Christ's Wounds](/thomas_wounds.jpg)

Thomas was a man who sought understanding, almost above all else. He wanted to know things concretely, and to prove them in his mind to be true. We see it plainly in the Gospel of John, when Thomas, in response to some of the disciples saying that they’ve seen the resurrected Lord, says, “Except I shall see in his hands the print of the nails, and put my finger into the print of the nails, and thrust my hand into his side, I will not believe.” (John 20:25). Almost as if in answer, Christ appears to the disciples eight days later, and offers Thomas the opportunity to see and feel the wounds of the crucifixion. At this, Thomas makes an act of faith, saying, “My Lord and my God!” (John 20:28).


We can learn two things from this interaction. The first is that we can trust and believe that the Lord will meet us where we are. Whether we are in doubt and despair, in faith and freedom, or somewhere in between, Jesus will touch our hearts in the way that they need to be touched. The second is that, so long as we are open to the grace of God, we can be sure He will pour it out to us. This was the moment that perhaps began to slowly transform Thomas’s heart, to soften it, to mold it more perfectly into an image of the Sacred Heart of Christ. For us, in moments like this, we may not understand it fully, but we know we have encountered the Lord. For Thomas, despite getting exactly what he wanted, it likely couldn’t have been more unbelievable – to his rational mind, that is. Through meeting us where we are, Christ is able to come alongside us and begin our transformation into holiness.


## The Ascension

![The Ascension](/thomas_ascension.jpg)

Moving forward, the disciples are present for the Ascension of Christ into heaven. He appears radiant and majestic, ascending regally into the clouds above. In the mural, Thomas is shielding his face – he must feel so unworthy to be in the presence of the Son of God, in all his majesty. And who wouldn’t feel that way? Thomas in that moment may have been realizing the privilege of the years spent in the presence of God, talking and relating to him as a brother. Or perhaps he may have been gaining a newfound appreciation for Jesus’ true nature. Whatever he was thinking at the time, one thing is clear: God’s glory was present to him, personally, in that moment. And the glory of God is transformative. It changes our hearts, animates us to do good, and gives sin a repugnant aroma. When we are touched personally by the glory of God, and experience his radiance, transforming power, and love, “old things are passed away; behold, all things are become new” (2 Corinthians 5:17).


## The Holy Spirit

![Holy Spirit](/thomas_hs.jpg)

To say nothing of the power of the Holy Spirit in St. Thomas’ transformation would be a gross omission. In the wide angle picture of all three murals, we can see that above the central mural rests an image of the Holy Spirit in the form of a dove. This same Holy Spirit, 10 days after Jesus returned to heaven, came to rest on each of the Apostles in the form of tongues of fire, animating each of them with the Resurrection power. In that moment, the Apostles’ hearts were filled with love, truth, and virtue, preparing them to radically and unequivocally bear witness to Christ. 
All that Thomas saw and experienced, from Jesus’ life and miracles, to his teaching and prayers, to his Passion and death, to his Resurrection and Ascension, led to this moment – he was to bear witness to the world of the truth of the Gospel: “For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have everlasting life” (John 3:16). And he would do so through the power of the Holy Spirit, animating him, convicting him, and setting his heart on fire. It is by this fire – lit in St. Thomas’ heart for the sake of the Kingdom and of souls – that he would ultimately receive the martyr’s crown.


## The Martyrdom of St. Thomas

![Martyrdom](/thomas_martyr.jpg)

And here we reach the final mural in the series, the martyrdom of St. Thomas. According to tradition, Thomas was martyred in India – killed with a spear while in prayer on a hill. Thomas, animated by the Holy Spirit with an intense desire to bring souls to Christ, traveled distant lands and, through his powerful witness, many were converted. The same man who once stubbornly needed to see and touch Jesus’ wounds to believe he was raised from the dead, later with reckless abandon brings the Gospel message to those who may take great offense at it. Through the slow but patient work of Jesus in his heart, over many years, Thomas came to a full conviction of Christ’s divinity, and all that it entails for souls. Thomas was transformed by love, and for love – by love, that is, by Christ, and for love, to bring that love to others.

Thomas assented to the martyr’s crown not by his own strength, but by the strength given to him by God. God worked in him powerfully, yet patiently, for many years, preparing his heart for the many trials He had planned for him. In allowing Thomas to touch His side, Christ humbly assented to Thomas’ need for empirical evidence of His Resurrection, lovingly meeting him where he was and drawing faith even from his stubbornness. In seeing Christ ascend to heaven, Thomas was filled with awe and wonder at the glory and power of God. Through the descent of the Holy Spirit upon him, his understanding, convictions, and belief were powerfully ignited for love of God and for souls. In and through all of his experiences, Thomas was transformed, and by God’s grace, humbly took up his life and laid it down for others, as Jesus himself did. St. Thomas, thus, is an example to all of us who walk the Christian life, of a man who, though flawed, allowed God to work in him. That same Jesus who met Thomas in his doubt is available for us, to allay our fears, give us strength, and perfect us in love. That same Father, to whom Christ Jesus ascended into the realms of endless day, is there to remind us of our sonship. And that same Holy Spirit, who animated Thomas, spurring him on to do mighty deeds in Jesus’ name and ultimately die for him, is available for us, that we might believe and follow the path to which God is calling us without fear, for we know He is always by our side: “I am with you always, even unto the end of the world” (Matthew 28:20).
